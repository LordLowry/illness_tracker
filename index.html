Know Before You Play

The Golf Courses

🏌️

Millennium

9 Apr (Twilight · 15:30) & 12 Apr (18 holes · 14:00)
Par 72 6,176m Slope 120 Architect: Martin Hawtree Opened: 2000

The most accessible of your three courses and the most popular in the Vilamoura portfolio. The front nine are largely open parkland before holes 3–7 wind through pine trees where the fairways narrow and accuracy becomes more important. The closing stretch around holes 17 and 18 features two lakes that demand a smart, patient approach — trying to be a hero here will cost you shots.

You're playing it twice — twilight on arrival day (a great warm-up) and again for the final full round on 12 April. Watch out for the par-3 6th, a 165-yard carry with water guarding the left of the green and a bunker to the right. The seventh is a twisting par-4 that catches the unwary. Greens have been in superb condition in recent months.

💡 Tip: Simple strategy always wins here — play for the middle of greens and avoid the water on the closing holes. Shared clubhouse with Laguna makes for a good post-round pint.
🌊

Laguna

10 Apr · 18 holes · 10:10
Par 72 6,121m Slope 129 Architect: Joseph Lee Opened: 1990

Laguna is the wildcard of the week — the most unusual course in Vilamoura. Designed by American architect Joseph Lee over flat, low-lying coastal terrain, it feels completely unlike its neighbours. With almost no trees, wide open fairways and water in play on 11 of 18 holes, it has a proper links character. Some have likened it to playing in Florida. The 79 bunkers are strategically placed throughout and the wind — when it blows — turns a manageable card into something quite demanding.

The front nine are shorter but trickier than they look. The back nine opens up and the driver comes out. The signature hole is the 12th — a par 3 over a lake with the Vilamoura skyline as your backdrop. It's a great selfie hole but a tough par. The 15th par-5 is one of the most talked-about on the course, with water seemingly everywhere.

💡 Tip: Pack extra balls — the water comes into play more than you expect. Wide tee shots are generally safe, but approach shots must be precise. Don't try to carry corners on the doglegs unless you're absolutely sure.
🌲

Pinhal

11 Apr · 18 holes · 10:10
Par 72 6,353m Slope 140 Pennink (1976) · Trent Jones Sr. (1985)

Pinhal is the oldest and, arguably, the most characterful of the three courses this week. Originally designed by Frank Pennink in 1976 and significantly revised by the legendary Robert Trent Jones Sr. in 1985 — one of only a select few European courses to bear the stamp of both architects. The course meanders through undulating parkland lined on almost every hole by canopies of mature umbrella and Atlantic pines. With the highest slope rating of the group (140), it demands accuracy from the tee above all else.

The front nine weaves through dense pine corridors — errant drives will be found but getting back to the fairway is the challenge. The back nine opens up with some fabulous sea views from the elevated sections, rewarding the brave golfer who has managed their card through the tighter front half. The par-3s here are widely considered the best in Vilamoura. The 14th and 15th are particular highlights.

💡 Tip: This is the toughest test of the week — accuracy off the tee is everything. Don't go chasing distance through the trees. Play conservatively through the front nine, attack on the back. The clubhouse has one of the best views in Vilamoura and great food — worth sitting on the veranda after.
